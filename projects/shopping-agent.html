agentic shopping agent

can an ai agent actually shop for you? ongoing research.

roleresearcher / builder
statusin progress
focusautonomous commerce

the question

every big ai lab is pitching "agents that buy things for you." but shopping is a deeply human, taste-driven, sometimes irrational act. what does it actually take for someone to trust an agent with their credit card — and what kinds of purchases are the right fit?

what i'm exploring

  • a taxonomy of purchase types — from "refill the same detergent" to "find a birthday gift for my mom" — and which ones are agent-ready
  • prototyping flows where the agent shows its reasoning before acting, not after
  • the role of taste — can an agent actually learn preferences, or does it flatten them into averages
  • failure modes — what happens when the agent gets it wrong, and how recoverable is the trust hit

what's emerging

the framing of "autonomous shopping" is often too ambitious. the more useful framing is "assisted decision-making that can also execute" — where the agent does the boring search work, presents a tight shortlist, and only acts autonomously within narrow, clearly-bounded permissions.

what's next

writing up findings, building a small prototype to test specific hypotheses, and continuing conversations with both users and retailers about where the actual willingness-to-delegate lives today.
