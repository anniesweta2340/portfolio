AI Governance and Advertising Economics in AI Mediated Commerce

How AI shopping agents reshape luxury consideration, sponsored recommendations, and trust in digital marketplaces.

The Question

As AI agents begin shaping what consumers compare, shortlist, and ultimately consider, luxury brands face a new strategic question: how do you monetize AI visibility without eroding brand equity? Our research asks whether AI primarily shapes consideration in luxury shopping, and how that changes advertising economics.

What I Contributed

  • Led governance and transparency analysis for a multi-method research project
  • Analyzed consumer trust risks around sponsored AI recommendations and disclosure
  • Developed the "cost per human" framing to explain why traditional impression-based advertising breaks when AI agents generate synthetic engagement
  • Translated findings into policy implications around FTC disclosure, human-verified metrics, and platform accountability
  • Built an interactive model to explore tradeoffs between monetization, transparency, and trust in AI mediated commerce, informed by platform experiments, consumer survey data, and financial modeling; designed the model as a conceptual tool to make governance tradeoffs visible rather than predictive — try simulation here

Experimental Approach

To understand what actually shapes AI shopping recommendations, our team tested how leading AI platforms responded to structured luxury shopping prompts.

  • Deployed 20 luxury shopping queries across ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, and Perplexity
  • Compared mechanisms including brand authority, metadata, platform routing, and early monetization signals
  • Tracked ranking position, routing behavior, and brand frequency to understand how AI shapes consideration sets

Heritage brands captured roughly 40–57% of top-ranked recommendations organically across platforms — suggesting that cultural authority currently matters more than paid placement.

Key Insights

AI is a consideration engine, not a discovery engine.
Brand authority dominates recommendations more than paid placement.
Sponsored disclosure creates a transparency paradox.
AI advertising shifts the metric from cost per impression to cost per human.

Industry Perspective

Conversations with Consumer Reports mentors reinforced that agentic commerce is still early — but the trust and measurement questions are already urgent.

01

Agentic commerce is still early, but monetization is expected to emerge as platforms test where sponsored placements belong inside AI interfaces.

02

AI is better suited for discovery, inspiration, and consideration than direct conversion — especially in luxury.

03

Luxury consumption is identity-driven, not purely transactional, which creates tension between AI intermediaries and brands that rely on control, exclusivity, and emotional connection.

04

Attribution remains a core challenge because AI may influence the top of the funnel long before a purchase happens in store or on a brand website.

Governance Takeaway

Even before sponsored AI recommendations scale, governance is economically relevant because AI already influences the consideration set. If platforms begin monetizing placement, existing consumer protection principles suggest paid recommendations would likely require disclosure — but traditional "sponsored" labels may not work well in conversational AI. Sustainable AI commerce will require standardized disclosure, human-verified engagement metrics, and transparency into why recommendations appear.

AI shifts advertising from buying impressions to buying consideration — but without governance, neither can be reliably measured.

Why It Matters

This project sits at the intersection of AI policy, consumer protection, and market design. It shows that the future of AI commerce is not only a product design problem, but also a trust and measurement problem. Platforms, brands, and policymakers will need new standards before AI-mediated recommendations can become a reliable advertising market.

Presented at Columbia Business School as part of the Digital Marketplaces Apprenticeship, in collaboration with Consumer Reports.
